Amazon Prime Video

User Needs Assessment

Qualitative UX Research

Timeline: July 2020 - 3 weeks

Learning project

Role: user interviews • data analysis • reporting

Background

I did this project as part of the course "Understanding User Needs" offered by the University of Michigan through Coursera. It was not commissioned by or for Amazon Prime Video in any way.

Amazon Prime Video is a subscription video-on-demand over-the-top streaming and rental service, offering movies, TV shows, documentaries, etc. In my observation, people I knew seemed to prefer some competitors of Amazon Prime Video in spite of higher subscription charges, and not just for the content being offered. The goal of this study is to discover viewers' needs and pain points when using Amazon Prime Video.

The overarching question:

What do users find difficult or frustrating while using Amazon Prime Video?

Course instruction: Select a website, app or service 'X' of your choice and ask the question "What do users find difficult or frustrating while using X?"

Business Opportunity:

Insights from this study will indicate problem areas that may be prioritized to deliver an improved experience to retain more viewers of Amazon Prime Video, translating to higher revenue.

Approach:

Semi-structured Interviews Data Extraction and Analysis Findings and insights Design suggestions

Course constraint: The course structure defined the first three steps of the approach. I came up with design suggestions as a bonus deliverable.

The generic research plan including participant criteria and recruitment methods was provided as part of the prompt in the course material.

Data Collection

The users of interest for this study were those who were-

  • current users of Amazon Prime Video i.e. having used the service at least once within the last 3 weeks.
  • conversational/fluent in English.
  • above 18 years of age.
  • not an immediate family member.

The participant criteria and recruitment methods were specified by the course instructor.

Amazon Prime Video is available on several types of devices. To learn about viewers' experiences, I would have preferred to interview 8-12 individuals per device type with a distinct interface. However, as the solo researcher, given the time constraints, I recruited 4 participants. One of them used the app on a smartphone, one on a laptop and two on TV. Audio recordings and anonymized responses were collected with informed consent. Participant recruitment was done by convenience sampling and semi-structured interviews of 45-60 minutes each were conducted remotely over video conferencing.

Using the event reconstruction technique for more reliable recollection, I asked questions about:

  • Use patterns
  • Preferences
  • Difficulties/frustrations encountered
  • Attempts to work around those problems

The course provided guidelines on the interview protocol. I have framed the specific questions myself.

  1. How often do you use Amazon Prime Video?
    1. How long have you had a subscription?
    2. How much do you watch at a time?
    3. Do you spend more time watching alone or with friends/family?
  2. In general, are you happy with Amazon Prime Video?
    1. Will you renew your subscription and why?
    2. Will you recommend Prime to a friend? Why?
    3. What do you think of the user interface?
  3. Think back to the most recent time when you watched a movie on Prime Video. Can you tell me about that experience?
    1. What device were you using?
    2. Had you already decided to watch that movie and searched for it, or did you browse through the titles and select this one?
    3. Did you have to interact with the interface once the movie had started? If so, when and why?
    4. Did you use subtitles? If so, how did you turn them on?
    5. Were you interrupted in any way while watching the movie? If so, what happened and how did you resolve the situation?
    6. Did you leave the movie unfinished at any point? If so, what did you do to resume later?
    7. What did you do when you finished the movie?
  4. Think back to the most recent time when you watched a TV show on Prime Video. Can you tell me about that experience?
    1. What device were you using?
    2. Had you already decided to watch that show and searched for it, or did you browse through the titles and select this one?
    3. Did you have to interact with the interface once the show had started? If so, when and why?
    4. Did you use subtitles? If so, how did you turn them on?
    5. Were you interrupted in any way while watching the show? If so, what happened and how did you resolve the situation?
    6. What did you do when you finished an episode?
    7. Think of a time when you wanted to navigate either forward or backward to another episode. How did you do that?
  5. Think of a time when you searched for a movie or TV show using the search bar. Can you tell me about that experience?
    1. What kind of keywords did you use? (name/genre/language etc)
    2. Did Prime offer suggestions as you were typing? If so, did you find them useful?
    3. Did you immediately find the movie/TV show that you had in mind? If not, why and what did you do?
    4. Have you ever searched for something that was not available on Prime? If so, how did you know it was unavailable?
    5. Did you find the other suggested results related and useful?
  6. Think of a time when you did not have a particular title in mind, but browsed the site to find something to watch. What was that like?
    1. Did you find the recommendations useful?
    2. Were you interested in a particular genre or language? If so, were you able to narrow your search effectively?
    3. Did you quickly find something to watch by focussing on a particular category or did you scroll through all the titles?
    4. What do you think of the way the titles are organized on the home page?
  7. Tell me about times while using Amazon Prime Video when you were confused or frustrated, when you made a mistake, or could not complete a task.
    1. What did you want to do?
    2. How did you go about doing it?
    3. Did you make a mistake? Why? What did you do to fix it? Was the mistake repeated?
    4. Did any of your actions yield an unexpected or unintended result? What did you do about it?
    5. Did you get stuck? What did you do to resolve the situation?
    6. What did you find confusing or frustrating? Why?

Here are the top level findings from the analysis of the affinity notes:

‘Recommendations’ and search results are the two main sources of content discovery, but they are tedious and slow to use on TV.

The large amount of content on the home page rarely provides value, but does waste time.

Users often need further information about content to make the decision to watch, but it is not readily available/discoverable.

The search function is unreliable for a user trying to determine if they will be able to watch what they have in mind with the Prime Video subscription that they have.

Resuming a half-watched movie or TV show may malfunction, and it is not easy to fix the problem by rewinding or forwarding.

There are both technological and interaction problems with audio and subtitles.

Key Findings and Insights

All four participants reported having spent a lot of time on selecting content to watch, sometimes giving up and exiting Prime Video as a result. I have selected two key findings related to this issue to expand on. Both these findings relate to the user’s experience from the time they come to Prime Video until they actually start streaming content.

1. Recommendations and search results are the two main sources of content discovery, but they are tedious and slow to use on TV.

It was found that most people only use the recommendation section and the search bar to find new content to watch, and the ‘Continue Watching’ section to resume half-watched movies or ongoing TV shows. The home page displays all of the content on Prime Video occupying many rows, but most people do not scroll past the first two rows which are the ‘Recommended’ and ‘Continue Watching’ sections. Those that do, reported having wasted a lot of time scrolling, trying to decide what to watch. One participant reported that they find the categorized titles on the home page irrelevant to their interests. Another reported using the search box to search in a particular genre or language rather than scrolling down to that category.

The recommendations and search box remain the go-to sources for content discovery across devices, but they are especially hard to use while using Prime Video on TV. The recommendations are displayed as a single row of title cards, so only a few titles are visible at a time. Scrolling horizontally on TV requires the user to click through each item up to the last item on the right, after which one new item at a time is revealed. People find this slow and restricting. Using the search box on TV is hard because typing is slowed down by the combination of a virtual keyboard and a remote control (usually for the Amazon Firestick) operating it.

Usability heuristics violated:

  • #7 Flexibility and efficiency of use
  • #8 Aesthetic and minimalist design

Design suggestions:

Recommendations might be better displayed in multiple rows with smooth scrolling. (It will require further research and testing on different devices to determine whether this will work, and with how many rows.)

Instead of navigating a virtual keyboard with arrow keys, it would be more efficient to use a remote control that can type alphabets with the number pad. The Amazon Firestick remote control does not do this.


2. The search function is unreliable for a user trying to determine if they will be able to watch what they have in mind with the Prime Video subscription that they have.

When a user has something in mind that they want to watch, they often do not know whether it is available with their Prime Video subscription or not. Titles appear in the live suggestions while typing in the search box even if they are not available. The search results are not clear about the availability of a title. One participant reported that when the show they were looking for did not show up in the results, they first thought they got the name wrong, and then assumed it was unavailable. Another reported scrolling through the results and assuming their movie was unavailable when they could not see it. On TV, search results include titles that are unavailable with a Prime user’s subscription, but available on another OTT platform. This is misleading and the user only finds out after clicking on the title card. Participants reported routinely doing a Google search on a title instead of using Prime Video’s search functionality to determine its availability on Prime.

Usability heuristics violated:

  • #4 Consistency and standards
  • #8 Aesthetic and minimalist design
  • #5 Error Prevention
  • #9 Recognize, diagnose & recover from errors

Design suggestions:

The user should not have to leave Prime Video to get the basic question of availability answered. Live suggestions and title cards in search results on TV should be clearly marked as unavailable. Finding out a title is not available after thinking that it is, is more unpleasant. Search results should clearly indicate unavailability to eliminate feelings of uncertainty.

Viewing suggestions displayed on the search results page should be well separated and labelled so they are not erroneously perceived as search results, whose irrelevance to the search was irritating according to one participant.

Reflection

About 2 years after I did this project, Amazon Prime Video rolled out a redesign that addressed some of the problems that this study identified.

  • A new side navigation to make it easier to find something to watch.
  • Icons on title cards that show whether content is included with an Amazon Prime subscription.
  • A new virtual keyboard layout that makes typing with a remote control faster.

I especially appreciate the keyboard layout modification as a solution that immediately improves the experience for all current users without the need for a hardware change.
