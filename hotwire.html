Hotwire.com

Usability testing of a flight booking website

Evaluative UX Research

Timeline: February 2022 - 4 weeks

Learning project

Role: task design • usability test moderation • qual+quant data analysis • reporting

Background

Hotwire.com is a travel booking website that offers cheap booking options for flights, cars and hotels for people’s travel plans as well as discounted vacation bundles.

Can experienced users of online travel booking sites use hotwire.com to plan their trips?

What problems do users encounter when trying to use the site to plan their trips?

Business Opportunity:

Insights from this study will indicate problem areas that may be prioritized to deliver an improved experience to attract and retain more users of hotwire.com, translating to higher revenue.

Methods:

Pre-test interview, Moderated usability test, Post-test questionnaire Data Analysis Reporting insights

The generic research plan including participant criteria and recruitment methods was provided as part of the prompt in the course material.

Test Participants

The principal audience identified for this study is “experienced users of online travel booking sites”. The recruitment criteria was defined as those adults who had-

  • bought a plane ticket online in the past year.
  • not used hotwire.com before.
  • have native or bilingual fluency in English. (since the site is in English)

To evaluate the site, I would have preferred to conduct test sessions with 5-7 individuals. However, as the solo researcher, given the time constraints, I recruited four participants by convenience sampling. All were habitual with using a laptop.

A short pre-test interview was conducted to characterize the participants’ experience with the site, their travel experience and booking preferences.

  1. Have you used hotwire.com before?
  2. Tell me about the last trip you planned.
    1. What do you usually use to plan your trip?
    2. What is your primary purpose for travelling?
    3. What is your primary concern?
    4. What is your budget?
  3. What information is the most important when you are planning your trip?
  4. How often do you travel?

Task Design

I wrote six tasks for the usability test involving different types of trips, seat classes, time constraints, price constraints, preferences and hotel booking. The tasks were designed to be

  1. realistic - something a typical user would actually do on the site to achieve a specific goal.
  2. verifiable - identifiable as successfully completed or not.
  3. not leading - not worded in a way that would influence how the participant approaches a task.
  4. in roughly increasing order of complexity

Task 1

Your manager asks you to help her plan a few trips for the company. She has heard of a website called “hotwire.com” that can help and encourages you to use it. Plan a trip for her to travel from Pune to New Delhi and back for under Rs 15,000 (or the next cheapest price) from January 16, 2022 to January 19, 2022. Any arrival/departure time is okay. How much does the trip cost? How long is each flight?

Task 2

4 people from the Bangalore office want to attend a conference in New Delhi from February 8, 2022 to February 10, 2022 . The conference will start at 11 am on February 8 and proceed until 6 pm each day. What is the cheapest total price of the trip?

Task 3

Your manager wants to join the Bangalore team for the conference in New Delhi (your office is in Pune), and then she wants to go to London for a week before returning to Pune. She plans to fly business class for the entire trip. What is the cheapest price for her trip? Will she be able to charge her laptop onboard the flights to and from London?

Task 4

The Bangalore office manager has a meeting in Mumbai on January 16, 2022 at 4 pm . He wants to leave on January 15, and can arrive anytime before noon the next day. How many flight options does he have?

Task 5

Help your manager book a place to stay from January 16 -18 . Find the top-rated 5-star hotel that has wifi for under Rs 5,500 /night in New Delhi. Note the address to share with some clients who will be meeting her there.

Task 6

You are flying to London with your parents and 4-year-old sister in the 2nd week of January to stay with cousins for a couple of months. Since your sister is little, you prefer a direct flight. (If not available, a layover of less than 3 hours is okay). You will be departing from Mumbai. You do not want to travel to the airport at odd hours, so you are looking for a flight leaving between 10 am and midnight . Your little sister gets fussy and your mother wants to keep her occupied watching cartoons during the flight. You want to sit in a window seat if available. The trip should not cost more than Rs. 4 lakh. How much luggage can you take at no extra cost? How long will you have to wait in layovers?

Test Sessions

I conducted a 60-75 minute long session with each test participant, including the pre-test interview and post-test questionnaire.

The participants were encouraged to think out loud while I observed them attempt the six test tasks on hotwire.com.

All four sessions were recorded using audio recording and screen capture software and later referred for analysis with informed consent from the participants. A logging sheet was used to note critical incidents and task success or failure.

The usability test was followed by a debrief interview to follow up on any moments of interest noted during task observation and the experience of using the site.

  1. Review parts of the test where the user struggled.
  2. Preferences:
    1. What did you think of the site?
    2. What did you like/dislike?
    3. Which parts of the site are most/least important to you?
  3. Changes: If you had 3 wishes to make this better for you, what would they be? Why?
  4. Understanding: How would you describe this site to a friend?
  5. Use cases: Under what circumstances would you use this? Why?

The standard System Usability Scale (SUS) [Brooke, John. (1995). SUS: A quick and dirty usability scale. Usability Eval. Ind.. 189.] was administered as the post-test questionnaire.

I had objectively defined the success criteria for each test task in the form of a "correct answer" the participant should have. I noted success/failure for each task on my logging sheets. During each task, I asked participants to tell me when they felt they had finished a task. I compared task success rates, time spent on each task and the number of errors that participants committed during each task.

I observed from these statistics that tasks 3, 5 and 6 seemed to have presented more problems overall. So once I had sorted all my observations into themes, I decided to prioritize those that were involved in tasks 3, 5 and 6.

The identified themes relate to the key findings discussed below.

Key Findings and Recommendations

The usability test revealed several issues that require fixing, however the overall sentiment towards the site was positive. All four participants said they would include hotwire.com in flight ticket price comparisons as they do other similar sites.

The SUS scores for perceived usability are scattered between 65(below average) and 95(very good); no conclusion can be drawn from these especially considering the small sample size.

1. All amounts are displayed in USD by default with an option to switch to only a few other currencies. This option does not appear on all pages.

Severity: 4/4     #Participants affected: 3/4

The initial search page shows a dropdown for selection of a different currency but other pages of the site only display the selected currency (USD by default) without an option to change it. This is a violation of Nielsen’s fourth heuristic, “Consistency and standards”.

Further, when the selection is available, it only features a limited list of currencies and the Indian Rupee is not one of them despite hotwire.com being operational in India. This study was conducted with Indian participants, all of whom were greatly frustrated by this and spent 4 minutes on average trying to look for an option to display prices in Indian Rupees. This is a violation of Nielsen’s second heuristic, “Match between system and real world”, rendering the participants helpless without an online converter or calculator.

Recommendation:

Set the default currency to the currency of the region from which the web request originates. In addition, allow the user to manually select a different currency if desired. This is seen in popular travel booking sites like MakeMyTrip.


2. Multicity option for flight type is not shown on the initial home page search, but appears later on the search results page among other filters.

Severity: 4/4     #Participants affected: 3/4

Flight trips with one or more intermediate destinations (not layovers) between the initial and final city are called “Multicity” trips. Hotwire.com does support booking such trips but the option is not listed among the search parameters on the initial search page. However, it is listed in the same dropdown on the following search results page. This is a violation of Nielsen’s fourth heuristic, “Consistency and standards”.

Of the four participants in this study, three were familiar with the term “Multicity” but assumed it was unavailable since it was not present on the initial search page. They proceeded to start to book 3 separate one-way trips instead of a 3-legged multicity trip, and expressed exasperation on discovering the option on the results page later in the task. One participant did not know the term and never discovered the option. This workaround was time consuming, required participants to keep track of individual flight information and most importantly, resulted in much higher total cost for the trip.

Recommendation:

The Multicity option should be made available on the initial search page also. This is standard practice on most popular trip booking sites like Google Flights, MakeMyTrip, etc.


3. Unavailability of in-flight entertainment/wifi/charging is not made explicitly clear.

Severity: 4/4     #Participants affected: 2/4

The availability of in-flight entertainment/wifi/charging in a particular flight is shown by icons in the right pane that opens when a search result is clicked on to inform the user’s decision about whether to select that flight. However, if any of these services is not available, the icon is simply absent. There is no explicit indication of absence of the service and the user must rely on their prior experience of having seen all three icons in that context to infer that a particular service is not offered on a flight. This is a violation of Nielsen’s sixth heuristic, “Recognition rather than Recall”.

Three of the four participants of the study encountered tasks with flights that did not offer some or all of these services, and all of them checked the right pane for related information. However, without a clear indication of unavailability, they resorted to assumptions based on their impression of the concerned airline.

Recommendation:

Unavailability could be indicated by greyed-out icons or icons with a diagonal line over them to support recognition. Leverage familiarity with the style of showing "off" status of aeroplane mode, WiFi, GPS etc in mobile phones.


4. The concept of Hot Rate Hotels is unclear.

Severity: 4/4     #Participants affected: 2/4

Hotwire.com offers a special service for hotel bookings at cheaper-than-market rates that they call “Hot Rate Hotels”. These offers are mixed in with standard rate listings in the search results. The offer entails that the user would not know the actual name of the hotel until booking is complete, however it is guaranteed to be one of three specified hotels in the same locality. None of the participants of this study were familiar with such a scheme and initially thought the “Hot Rate” tag simply indicated the lowest price available. Two of the four participants did not figure it out and abandoned the task. The other two who did eventually realize that the “Hot Rate” offer would not let them know the exact hotel address before booking took an average of 11 minutes to complete the task.

The scheme was explained on each Hot Rate listing page and even the two participants who gave up had read the description, but it appears that it was not clear enough.

Recommendation:

A redesign of the hotel listings is required to make the concept of Hot Rate hotels more apparent and to properly separate them from the standard listings.


5. The price range filter for hotel search is a slider with granularity $1 which is difficult to manipulate with precision.

Severity: 3/4     #Participants affected: 3/4

The Hotel search results page provides a way to filter results by price range in the form of a slider. The slider moves in $1 increments over a large range like $200+.

All four participants of the study had to move the slider slowly and carefully to apply their desired price cap with precision, over-sliding and correcting a few times. While three were not particularly annoyed by this, one participant who took longer than the others to get it right did express irritation.

Recommendation:

A slider that moves in larger increments will support the physical capability for coarser motor control. However, checkboxes to select relatively broad price ranges as on Amazon, or numeric input fields would be more suitable input methods for price range.


6. Arrival/departure time filters for flights are rigid and error-prone.

Severity: 3/4     #Participants affected: 3/4

The Flights search results page has a left pane for filters including arrival and departure time. These are provided for broad 4-7 hour periods - Early Morning, Morning, Afternoon, Evening.

Three of the four participants of the study struggled to apply the right filters to correctly narrow down search results to the desired arrival and/or departure times. One participant expressed that these time periods, though cleanly categorized by the names of times of the day, had boundaries at odd times that do not match the way they think about parts of the day for travel purposes. This is a violation of Nielsen’s second heuristic, “Match between system and real world”. The participant expressed a desire to be able to freely enter any time period as arrival / departure time filters.

Recommendation:

Further study is required to determine if the current design is indeed mismatched with users’ requirements. If so, replace rigid 4-7 hour periods by free time period input as arrival/departure time filters or by periods that are found to be suitable.

Limitations

This study was conducted with a very small sample size of only four participants. A study with a larger sample size with representation along dimensions like frequency of using online booking sites and special requirements affecting booking and travel would likely provide better insight.
